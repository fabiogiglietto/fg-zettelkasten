# From the Wild West to the Walled Garden

## Summary

Giglietto and Puschmann offer a periodized history of researcher access to social media data, using Twitter/X as a focal case to argue that the contraction of academic data access is a systemic, cross-platform phenomenon rather than the idiosyncratic fallout of Musk's acquisition. They identify three phases—an open "Wild West" (2006–2020), a brief "Golden Age" under the Academic Research API (2020–2023), and a post-2023 paywalled era—and situate parallel retrenchments at Meta (CrowdTangle), Reddit, and TikTok as evidence of an industry-wide enclosure. They then assess Article 40 of the EU Digital Services Act as a paradigm shift that reframes data access from a discretionary platform favor into a legal right tied to studying systemic risks, while cataloging its implementation gaps and arguing that independent platform research must be treated as a public good requiring sustained public investment.

## Key Contributions

- A three-phase periodization (Wild West → Golden Age → walled garden) that gives the field a shared vocabulary for the evolution of data access.
- Reframing platform data restrictions as a systemic, cross-platform pattern rather than isolated corporate decisions.
- A critical appraisal of DSA Article 40 as both a regulatory breakthrough and a geographically and structurally limited remedy.
- A policy and infrastructural agenda — academic consortia, public investment, and multi-method approaches (APIs, data donations, browser-based collection) — for sustaining independent research.

## Methods

A historical and conceptual case study of Twitter/X's successive data access regimes, comparatively contextualized against Meta, Reddit, and TikTok, combined with a policy analysis of the DSA — especially Article 40 — and its implementation challenges.

## Findings

- Twitter/X's access history cleanly periodizes into open, academic-privileged, and commercially paywalled eras.
- The same retrenchment pattern is visible across Meta, Reddit, and TikTok, indicating industry-wide closure.
- The DSA establishes a legal entitlement to data but is undermined by inconsistent platform compliance and stark knowledge and resource asymmetries between platforms and researchers.
- Burdens fall hardest on early-career scholars, civil society, and Global South researchers, producing a "data abyss" outside the EU's jurisdictional reach.
- Progress on data transparency is non-linear and fragile, dependent on institutional rather than platform-dependent solutions.
