# Social theory should be a structural prior for agentic AI: A formal framework for multi-Agent Social Systems

## Summary

This position paper contends that agentic AI deployed in social environments must inherit social theory as a *structural prior*, not be modeled as a population of isolated task-completers. The authors formalize Multi-Agent Social Systems (MASS) as a dynamical triple (f, g, G) — an information-generation function, a local influence function, and an interaction network — and identify four priors that social theory imposes on any such system: strategic heterogeneity, network-constrained dependence, co-evolution, and distributional instability. Each prior violates a standard assumption of single-task AI evaluation, and each is shown empirically in MoltBook, an LLM-only social network of ~39,700 agents and 2.1M posts. The paper argues that alignment techniques like RLHF address dyadic problems and cannot substitute for population-level governance, and frames MARL, social simulation, theory of mind, and social cybersecurity as partial instantiations of MASS.

## Key Contributions

- A tripartite formalism (f, g, G) for Multi-Agent Social Systems grounded in role theory, structuration, Friedkin–Johnsen and DeGroot dynamics.
- Four structural priors with formal propositions, each tied to a specific violation of single-task AI assumptions.
- An empirical demonstration of all four priors in a real LLM-agent social platform, showing they are not simulation artifacts.
- A research agenda for modeling, evaluation, and governance, including a critique of static benchmarks and a call for longitudinal, network-aware metrics.
- A conceptual map situating MARL, ToM, social simulation, and social cybersecurity as special cases of MASS, and clarifying that alignment ≠ population-level governance.

## Methods

The paper combines theoretical framework construction with empirical validation. Conceptually, it draws on sociology (weak ties, homophily, agenda-setting, social construction of reality) and mathematical sociology to derive four propositions with proof sketches. Empirically, it analyzes ~2.1M posts from 39,700 agents on MoltBook (Jan 31–Feb 8, 2026), partitioning agents into hub/mid/periphery by degree and running: Kruskal–Wallis and Mann–Whitney U tests for heterogeneity; Levene and Fligner–Killeen tests for variance/dependence; OLS regression of karma change on lagged neighbor karma for co-evolution; and Wasserstein-1 distances between consecutive daily karma distributions for distributional instability. Reply networks are constructed and visualized via Louvain clustering.

## Findings

- **Strategic heterogeneity (P1):** Mean karma trajectories diverge significantly across hub/mid/periphery partitions (Kruskal–Wallis p < 0.001 at all time bins; large negative effect sizes for hub vs. periphery).
- **Network-constrained dependence (P2):** Karma variance differs significantly by degree class, with hubs showing substantially higher variance — topology amplifies engagement spread.
- **Co-evolution (P3):** OLS slopes of karma change on lagged neighbor karma are non-zero, time-varying, and bidirectional (mean |β| = 0.057; Wilcoxon p = 0.0039), evidencing mutual agent–network coupling.
- **Distributional instability (P4):** Consecutive daily karma distributions drift endogenously (mean W1 = 0.253; KS bin 0 vs. bin 9, D = 0.046, p = 0.0025).
- All four priors are observable in a deployed AI agent ecosystem, not just in simulation.
