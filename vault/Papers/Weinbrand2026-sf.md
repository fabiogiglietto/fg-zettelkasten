# Framing the future of search: A discourse analysis of Google’s AI Overviews

## Summary

This paper investigates how Google's AI Overviews (AIOs) — generative AI summaries launched atop search results in May 2024 — are discursively framed during their first year of deployment. Drawing on a corpus of 613 English-language texts from Google itself, technology journalists, and SEO marketers, the authors argue that AIOs constitute a contested redefinition of search rather than a neutral technical upgrade. Google promotes AIOs as a seamless, helpful evolution while hedging through "experimental" labels; journalists frame them as epistemically risky and prone to hallucination; and SEO marketers anxiously interpret them through the lens of lost traffic and visibility. The paper situates AIOs within a hybrid platform/infrastructure logic that extends the "Googlization of knowledge" into a generative epistemic regime.

## Key Contributions

- One of the first systematic discourse analyses of AI Overviews across multiple stakeholder groups during the rollout year.
- Demonstrates the analytic value of combining Leximancer concept mapping with critical discourse analysis for studying platform discourse at scale.
- Extends platformisation and "Googlization of knowledge" theories to generative AI search, theorising AIOs as a hybrid platform-infrastructure phenomenon.
- Identifies systematic discursive exclusions — users, environmental costs, and ethical/democratic implications — as a structural feature of industry framing.
- Reframes Google's role as shifting from indexical gatekeeper to generative epistemic authority.

## Methods

A mixed-methods discourse analysis combining computational and qualitative approaches. The authors web-scraped 613 English-language texts (May 2024–May 2025) referencing "Google AI Overview," classified by domain into three actor categories: Google (30), tech journalism (253), and marketing/SEO (330). Leximancer was used to extract themes, concept co-occurrences, and sentiment likelihoods over 2–3 sentence context blocks. Representative texts were then analysed qualitatively using Fairclough's three-level model (text, discourse practice, sociocultural context) alongside van Leeuwen's framework.

## Findings

- Four dominant thematic frames emerged: (1) Generative AI Technologies and AI-Platform Wars; (2) Reconfiguring Search — Let Google Do the Searching for You; (3) Commercial Implications of AIOs; and (4) AI Overviews — Utopia versus Dystopia.
- Sentiment diverges sharply by actor: Google neutral-positive (5% favourable vs 4% unfavourable), SEO marketers engaged but conflicted (66% vs 52%), journalists cautiously critical (22% vs 40%).
- Concept salience differs: Google foregrounds Gemini, model, people; SEO actors foreground search, engine, content; journalists foreground Google, search, company.
- Rhetorical registers contrast: Google's "supersmart, superfast librarian" metaphor and language of quality and seamlessness versus journalists' invocations of "disaster," hallucination examples (glue on pizza, eating rocks), and SEO reports of dramatic clickthrough drops driving a pivot to Generative Engine Optimization (GEO).
- Users' perspectives, environmental costs of LLMs, and democratic/ethical implications are largely absent across all three actor groups.
